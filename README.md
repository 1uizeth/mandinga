# Mandinga Protocol

### *A Solidarity Savings Primitive for Everyone*

*Lightpaper v0.1 — February 2026*

*Mandinga Protocol is a privacy-first, self-custodial savings primitive that gives anyone on Earth access to competitive, real-world yield — without a bank account, without KYC, and without surrendering custody of their funds. Built on an ancient human coordination mechanism — the savings circle — it uses yield-routed capital, structural enforcement, and a peer solidarity market to democratise the single most powerful wealth-building force available: compounding on a lump sum.*

## 1. The Problem

### 1.1 Compounding Is the Most Unequal Force in Finance

Yield on savings is, at its core, a function of capital size and time. The more capital you start with, and the earlier you access a lump sum to put to work, the more you benefit from compounding. This is not a bug — it is the arithmetic of exponential growth. But it means that the wealthiest savers structurally earn the highest returns, not because of superior skill or risk tolerance, but simply because they already have capital.

The person depositing \$50 a month earns yield on \$50. The person who inherited \$50,000 earns yield on \$50,000 from day one. The gap compounds. Over a lifetime, the difference is not marginal — it is the difference between financial precarity and financial stability.

Traditional finance has no answer to this problem. Banks offer savings accounts but apply no mechanism to give smaller savers access to lump-sum capital earlier. Wealth managers serve those who are already wealthy. Microfinance lends at punishing rates. The structural advantage of having capital upfront remains entirely inaccessible to the majority of the world\'s population.

### 1.2 The World Invented the Answer Thousands of Years Ago

Communities across every continent independently invented the same solution: the rotating savings and credit association. It goes by many names — chama in East Africa, tanda in Mexico, hui in China, paluwagan in the Philippines, equb in Ethiopia, susu in West Africa, stokvel in South Africa, gye in Korea. The same primitive, invented everywhere humans organised themselves economically.

The mechanic is simple. A group of people contribute a fixed amount regularly. The pooled total rotates to one member per round. Everyone eventually receives the same lump sum — but the member who receives it first gets the compounding advantage of deploying a larger amount earlier. The member who receives it last has effectively saved patiently while others benefited.

What makes this work is not charity. It is solidarity — horizontal, mutual, between equals. The group is the mechanism. The community IS the collateral. No bank. No credit bureau. No identity verification.

According to the World Bank, roughly 25% of adults in sub-Saharan Africa report having used a savings circle. Estimates suggest hundreds of millions of people globally participate in some form of rotating savings. This is not a niche instrument — it is the dominant savings technology for much of humanity.

### 1.3 The Structural Failure Mode

ROSCAs and savings circles have one catastrophic vulnerability: the organiser. In every traditional implementation, one person holds the pot, decides the rotation order, and is trusted by all members not to abscond. When that person disappears — or when an early-payout member stops contributing after receiving the lump sum — the circle collapses. Members waiting for later payouts lose everything they contributed.

This is not a rare edge case. It is the reason ROSCAs remain informal, local, and trust-constrained. You can only participate with people you know personally, in close geographic proximity, with strong social accountability. The social trust that makes them work is also the ceiling that prevents them from scaling.

Every attempt to formalise ROSCAs has tried to solve this with reputation systems, identity verification, or legal enforcement. Each solution imports the exact surveillance and gatekeeping apparatus that makes traditional finance inaccessible to the very people who need savings circles in the first place.

### 1.4 Case Study: What Happened to the Consórcio in Brazil

Brazil offers the clearest and most instructive example of what happens when a solidarity savings primitive is institutionalised. The consórcio — the Brazilian expression of the rotating savings circle — has existed since the 1960s and today reaches over 10 million active participants. Understanding what it became, and how, is essential to understanding what Mandinga Protocol is designed to prevent.

#### The Original Promise: Better Than Financing

The consórcio began as a genuinely attractive alternative to traditional bank lending. In a country where the Selic rate currently sits at 15% per year and mortgage financing rates run 11--12% annually on top of inflation correction, the consórcio offered something structurally different: no interest, only a fixed administration fee unaffected by Selic movements. For a R\$200,000 real estate credit, the total administration fee runs 15--20% of the credit value spread across the contract — dramatically cheaper than compound interest over the same period, where the equivalent financing could cost 60--100% more in total outlay.

This advantage is real. From January to April 2025 alone, new consórcio enrolments grew 19.1% year-on-year, with the real estate segment up 41%. The market expects to surpass 11 million active participants in 2026 with 25% segment growth. But cheaper than financing in Brazil is a low bar. And the comparison conceals a misalignment that is foundational: the administration fee is paid in full regardless of when you are contemplated. Contemplated in month two or month 118 of a 120-month plan, you pay the same total fee. The fee structure rewards the administradora identically whether it serves you early or late.

#### The Auction Mechanic: Where Solidarity Ended

The original ROSCA logic distributes lump-sum access through rotation — every member receives the pool once, timing determined by lot or agreed sequence. The consórcio preserved this through its monthly sorteio (lottery draw). But it added a second contemplation path that broke the solidarity logic entirely: the lance, a competitive bid auction held at each monthly assembly.

Any member can offer a lance — a percentage of the credit value — to win early contemplation. The highest bidder wins. This means the member with the most capital to deploy wins the timing advantage every assembly. The structural benefit that gave ROSCAs their purpose — giving everyone equal access to lump-sum capital in turn — became purchasable by those who already had capital. The wealthiest participant in the circle gets the money first. Every month. Indefinitely.

The lance embutido added further complexity. This mechanism lets members bid using part of their own carta de crédito as the offer — a member with a R\$100,000 carta can offer a 30% embedded lance of R\$30,000 to accelerate contemplation, receiving only R\$70,000 when contemplated. The intent was to democratise auction access for members without large capital reserves. The effect was a new layer of opaque calculation: participants must weigh reducing their effective credit against accelerating contemplation, based on group dynamics they cannot fully observe.

#### The Secondary Market: From Savings to Speculation

Once institutionalised, the consórcio developed a secondary market for cotas — quota positions — that transformed the product entirely. A contemplated cota commands a premium called ágio. In practice, the ágio on a recently contemplated quota runs 25--35% of the credit value in early group months, declining as the group matures. A R\$200,000 carta contemplada might be sold on the secondary market for R\$40,000--R\$70,000 in entry premium, with the buyer assuming remaining installment obligations.

This spawned an entire class of consórcio strategists — consultants selling strategies for leveraging net worth through coordinated quota acquisition, contemplation timing, and secondary market arbitrage. The strategies are genuinely sophisticated: acquire multiple lower-value quotas to maximise lottery chances; target early contemplation by sorteio and sell the carta at ágio before the premium decays; use lance embutido to win contemplation without capital outlay and flip immediately; build patrimonial leverage structures where contemplated cartas serve as collateral for further credit. These strategies are rational for those who know them. They are completely invisible to ordinary participants, and they extract from the group pool in ways that ordinary members neither understand nor consent to.

By 2025, the consórcio system issued over 5.16 million new quotas in a single year. It is no longer a community savings tool. It is an institutionalised credit market using rotating pool mechanics as its legal structure, with secondary markets, strategic arbitrage, and information asymmetries that systematically favour sophisticated actors over ordinary ones.

### 1.5 The Failure Mode Was Never Fixed; It Was Replicated

Whether a ROSCA fails informally through organiser fraud, or institutionally through auction mechanics and secondary market opacification, the root cause is the same: someone outside the group holds power that should belong to the collective. The circle breaks not because cooperative logic is flawed, but because an institution introduced single points of control that the cooperative logic was never designed to accommodate.

Every structural fix attempted by institutions reproduces the problem it claims to solve. Reputation systems gate access on identity and credit history. Auction mechanics gate timing on capital. Secondary market transfers gate continuity on administradora approval. Legal enforcement gates dispute resolution on jurisdictional access. Each intervention extends institutional sovereignty over a mechanism whose power derived precisely from operating outside institutional sovereignty.

### 1.6 DeFi Has Not Solved This Either

Decentralised finance has produced remarkable infrastructure for yield, lending, and capital markets. But it has not produced a savings primitive for ordinary people. The reasons are structural:

-   Yield-bearing stablecoins with competitive real-world rates require KYC gating at the issuance layer — tokenised treasuries are accessible only to professional clients and licensed resellers.

-   Genuinely permissionless DeFi yield (sDAI, Aave, Compound) derives from crypto-native sources — borrowing demand, protocol incentives — that are volatile and collapse in downturns.

-   Undercollateralised lending in DeFi requires either identity exposure or reputation scoring — both of which recreate surveillance-based access control.

-   Governance is token-weighted — one dollar one vote — which means financial influence is proportional to wealth, the opposite of cooperative logic.

Most critically: DeFi has never built a solidarity primitive. Every existing protocol is designed around individual financial maximisation. There is no primitive whose success metric is community resilience, whose risk model is mutualized, or whose governance has structural limits on financial influence.

Where DeFi has attempted to encode savings circle mechanics on-chain, it has typically replicated the consórcio mistake: preserving the rotation mechanic while missing the point. On-chain ROSCAs built to date treat the pool as a coordination layer without asking whether the pool itself could generate yield between rounds, or whether the selection mechanic should encode fairness principles that protect all members equally — including those who cannot bid competitively.

The savings circle mechanic — the most widely used community savings technology in human history — does not exist in any meaningful form on-chain. And the one institutional attempt to build it at scale in the 21st century transformed it into exactly the thing it was designed to replace.

## 2. The Philosophy: Solidarity, Not Charity

Mandinga Protocol is built on a distinction that matters: the difference between charity and solidarity.

  ----------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------
  **Charity Logic**                         **Solidarity Logic**

  Hierarchical — donors on top            Horizontal — mutual aid between equals

  Sustained by the goodwill of donors       Sustained by mutual interdependence

  Decisions made by boards and funders      Decisions made by the community

  Treats symptoms of inequality             Addresses root causes by building alternatives

  Success = amount donated, number served   Success = self-determination, reduced inequality

  Disconnected donations                    Community-owned infrastructure
  ----------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------

Charity DeFi would give yield to the poor as a subsidy. Solidarity DeFi gives everyone the structural mechanism to access lump-sum capital on equal terms, governed by the community, with surplus flowing back to members.

This maps directly onto the Defipunk values articulated by the Ethereum Foundation: finance that is permissionless, censorship-resistant, privacy-first, self-custodial, and open source. Not finance that is marginally better than TradFi — finance that could not exist without Ethereum.

The three principles that guide every design decision in Mandinga Protocol are:

-   Mutualized risk: when a member faces difficulty, the circle responds collectively, not algorithmically.

-   Structural enforcement over surveillance: the protocol enforces correct behaviour through incentive design, not identity tracking or permanent reputation marks.

-   Governance with limits on financial influence: participation rights are not proportional to deposits. The cooperative surplus flows to all members, not to the largest holders.

## 3. The Solution: Mandinga Protocol

### 3.1 The Savings Account: Your Base Layer

Every member begins with a self-custodial savings account. Depositing a dollar-stable asset (USDC or equivalent) into Mandinga immediately begins earning yield. That yield comes from real-world sources — short-duration government instruments, money market positions, and established DeFi savings rates — routed automatically by the protocol\'s yield engine. No manual management. No yield strategy decisions required.

The savings account is the foundation. All features of Mandinga Protocol sit on top of it. You can use the savings account alone and it will quietly compound for you. The savings circle is an optional feature you activate when you are ready.

### 3.2 The Savings Circle: Yield Leverage as a Feature

When you activate the savings circle feature, the protocol places you in a circle appropriate to your balance size. Every active circle has a defined pool size, a group of members, and a rotation schedule. Each round, one member is selected to receive the full pool payout. That payout is deposited directly into their savings position — not into their withdrawable wallet. It compounds immediately at the full pool size.

The key mechanic: the payout is principal-locked. The member who receives it has access to the yield it generates, but the principal itself serves as the installment obligation back to the circle. Over subsequent rounds, that obligation is automatically satisfied from the member\'s ongoing savings balance. At the end of the circle\'s duration, the principal has returned to the pool and been distributed to all remaining members.

What the member who received the early payout keeps is the yield differential — the additional compounding they earned by having the full pool amount working for them from the moment of selection, rather than contributing installments and receiving the pool only at the end.

**Selection and Fairness**

Selection order within a circle can follow multiple models, all of which preserve the collective fairness of the mechanism over a full rotation cycle. Options include random selection with verifiable on-chain randomness, auction-based selection where members bid a small premium for early selection (which flows to the solidarity pool), or sequential bidding where expressed preferences determine order. Every member receives the full pool exactly once per full rotation cycle regardless of selection order.

#### The Principal Lock: Structural Enforcement Without Surveillance

There is no default risk in Mandinga Protocol. The installment obligation is not a social promise or a credit commitment. It is a structural property of the savings account itself: your savings balance must always maintain a minimum corresponding to your outstanding circle obligation. If your balance falls below that threshold, circle participation is automatically paused until the balance is restored. The circle does not fail. The member\'s position is held in suspension until they are able to continue.

This design choice eliminates the need for reputation systems, governance intervention for defaults, or identity-based accountability. The enforcement is in the architecture, not in surveillance.

## 4. The Solidarity Market: Vouching as Passive Income

Circle size is determined by savings balance. This is the structural guarantee that makes the principal lock work. A member with a \$300 balance can participate in circles up to a proportionate pool size. A member with \$2,000 can access larger circles with higher yield leverage premiums.

This creates an access gap for members whose savings are growing but not yet large enough for meaningful circle participation. The Solidarity Market is the mechanism that closes this gap — and creates a genuinely new form of passive solidary income for those with larger balances.

### 4.1 Vouching: How It Works

A member with a larger balance can extend a vouch to another member. The vouch designates a portion of the voucher\'s balance as backing for the vouched member\'s circle participation, effectively allowing them to join a circle tier larger than their own balance would qualify them for. The vouched member accesses greater yield leverage. The voucher earns:

-   An interest fee on the leveraged amount, paid by the vouched member from their yield earnings.

-   A share of the payout differential when the vouched member is selected — a portion of the compounding advantage they helped make possible.

The vouch is economically real. The vouched portion of the voucher\'s balance is deployed on behalf of the vouched member and cannot be withdrawn while the vouch is active. If the vouched member\'s savings balance falls below the circle threshold and participation pauses, the vouch obligation also pauses. The voucher has full visibility into the status of all active vouches.

### 4.2 Two Strategies, One Protocol

Mandinga Protocol offers two complementary participation strategies that serve different member profiles and risk appetites:

  --------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------
  **Active Circle Participation**         **Passive Solidarity (Vouching)**

  Join a circle matched to your balance   Deploy balance as vouching capital across multiple members

  Receive full pool payout in one round   Earn interest plus payout share from vouched members

  Higher upside when selected early       Lower upside, diversified across multiple vouches

  Capital tied to circle duration         Capital available to redeploy as vouches mature

  Maximises personal yield leverage       Amplifies others\' access while generating passive return
  --------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------

The solidarity market is not simply a financial instrument. It encodes the core principle of the cooperative model: those who have more capacity can amplify the access of those building toward capacity, and both benefit from the arrangement. The surplus flows toward the community, not toward extraction.

## 5. User Flows and Real Use Cases

### 5.1 Amara — Lagos, Nigeria

*Amara is a seamstress. She earns inconsistently but saves deliberately. She has never had a bank account that paid meaningful interest, and she has participated in a local ajo (rotating savings circle) for years — but the organiser disappeared with the pot two years ago. She lost three months of contributions.*

Amara deposits \$120 into Mandinga Protocol using a mobile wallet connected to the protocol\'s interface. Her savings account immediately begins earning yield on the full balance.

She activates the savings circle feature. The protocol places her in a 10-member circle with a \$1,200 pool — \$120 per member per round. In round three, she is selected. Her savings balance shows \$1,200. She watches it compound at the full pool rate. Over the following seven rounds, her installment obligations are satisfied automatically from her balance.

At the end of the full circle, Amara\'s principal has returned to the pool. But she has earned the yield on \$1,200 since round three — far more than she would have earned saving \$120 alone. More importantly, she experienced no risk of losing contributions to an organiser. There was no organiser. The protocol was the organiser.

She now understands the circle mechanics well. She invites her sister to join. She vouches for her sister to access a larger circle than her sister\'s initial \$80 balance would permit. She earns a small ongoing interest payment from her sister\'s yield, and a share of the payout differential when her sister is selected. Solidarity, made economically concrete.

### 5.2 Rafael — São Paulo, Brazil

*Rafael is a delivery driver. He owns his motoç — the motorcycle he uses for work — and is saving to upgrade it. He knows the consórcio world well. Two years ago, he joined a motorcycle consórcio with a R\$15,000 carta de crédito, paying R\$280/month. He is in month 19 of a 60-month group. He has not been contemplated. He has watched other members win contemplation through lances he could not match. He has calculated that at his current pace, he will likely be contemplated somewhere between month 35 and 50 — and will have paid the full administration fee regardless of when that happens. He is not angry. This is just how consórcios work. But he is starting to wonder whether the deal was as good as it looked.*

Rafael has R\$3,200 in savings outside the consórcio — money he cannot put toward a lance without liquidating his emergency reserve. A friend tells him about Mandinga Protocol. He is sceptical. He has heard of crypto but associates it with speculation and volatility, not with saving for a motorcycle.

What changes his mind is the framing: not 'invest in crypto' but 'join a savings circle that cannot be stolen from, where no one can outbid you for timing, and where your money earns yield while it waits.' He converts his R\$3,200 savings equivalent to a dollar-stable asset and deposits into Mandinga. His balance starts earning yield immediately.

He joins a circle sized for his balance. Selection is by verifiable lottery — there is no lance mechanic. There is no auction. No one in the circle can purchase earlier access. In round six, he is selected. His position jumps to the full pool value. He watches yield accrue on the larger amount. He does not receive the principal — it is locked, returning to the circle through his ongoing contributions — but the yield on the larger amount accrues entirely to him from that moment forward.

Rafael is still in his original consórcio. He is not angry about it — it will eventually deliver a carta de crédito, and the total cost really is lower than financing. But for his next vehicle, he will not use a consórcio. He will use Mandinga. He knows now that cheaper than financing was never the bar. The bar is: does the mechanism serve me, or does it serve the institution?

### 5.3 Sofia — Buenos Aires, Argentina

*Sofia is a software developer with \$3,000 saved in dollar-stable assets. Argentina\'s inflation has taught her not to hold local currency. She is not looking to speculate — she wants stable, real-world yield without KYC requirements she does not trust.*

Sofia deposits into Mandinga Protocol. Her balance qualifies her for larger circle tiers. She participates actively for two cycles, experiencing the yield leverage mechanic firsthand.

In her third year of using the protocol, she decides to shift her strategy. She has \$5,000 in savings and begins deploying a portion as vouching capital across eight members of the protocol\'s community — several of them referred by members she knows, several completely unknown to her. She evaluates vouching opportunities based on the vouched member\'s savings history within the protocol (duration, consistency) — not their identity.

Sofia now earns passive income from eight active vouches simultaneously. Her capital amplifies the circle access of eight people who would otherwise be in smaller circles. When any of them is selected, she receives a portion of their yield differential. This is not charity. It is mutualized solidarity — structured as a market.

### 5.4 A Diaspora Community — Coordinated Across Borders

*A family network spans three countries: members in Nairobi, London, and Toronto. They have historically coordinated a family chama using WhatsApp and informal trust. Twice in the last decade, members have defaulted on contributions. The emotional and financial cost to the group was significant.*

The family moves their circle to Mandinga Protocol. Each member deposits their monthly contribution amount as their savings balance. They join the same circle, linked by a shared circle identifier that they generate and distribute privately. The protocol does not know they are a family. It sees a circle of members with consistent savings behaviour.

The circle operates across three time zones, three currencies (all bridged to dollar-stable assets at entry), and three jurisdictions — without any of these creating friction. The rotation operates automatically. Selection is provably random. When the Toronto member receives the payout, the Nairobi and London members\' installment contributions continue automatically.

The family has no organiser. They have a protocol. The social trust that made the original chama work is preserved — they chose each other, they vouch for each other, they govern together. But the structural vulnerability that broke it twice has been removed.

### 5.5 Grace Period and Community Vouching for New Members

*A newcomer arrives with \$80 — below the minimum meaningful circle size. She cannot afford to build her balance quickly. A community member who has participated in Mandinga for two years vouches for her entry into a \$500 circle.*

The voucher extends \$420 of backing from their balance. The newcomer\'s \$80 plus the \$420 vouch creates a qualifying position. The newcomer joins the circle. Over subsequent rounds, as she adds to her savings balance, the vouch obligation gradually reduces — her own balance grows into the position the voucher initially held on her behalf.

The voucher earns interest on the \$420 backing and a share of the payout differential when the newcomer is selected. The newcomer earns yield leverage she could not have accessed alone. Both benefit. The relationship is transparent within the protocol and opaque to the outside world.

This is graduation. Not charity. Not a loan. A structured solidarity arrangement where a more established member amplifies a newcomer\'s access, economically incentivised to do so, with no identity information exchanged.

## 6. What Makes This Defipunk

The Ethereum Foundation\'s test for Defipunk DeFi is whether the thing could exist without Ethereum. Mandinga Protocol fails this test in the best possible way: it cannot exist anywhere else.

-   Could it exist on traditional banking rails? No. Banks require identity, report to credit bureaus, are geographically constrained, and extract surplus for shareholders.

-   Could it exist as a standard DAO? No. DAOs are token-weighted and fully transparent. Full transparency destroys the social grace that makes cooperative savings work.

-   Could it exist with a centralised yield manager? No. A human treasurer recreates the single point of failure that makes ROSCAs break.

-   Could the privacy layer exist on a standard public blockchain? No. Public ledgers would expose every member\'s balance and contribution history to the world, permanently and irreversibly.

-   Could the solidarity market exist without decentralised enforcement? No. Vouching requires trustless structural guarantees that the backed position cannot be withdrawn while the vouch is active.

Mandinga Protocol is also specifically aligned with the Ethereum Foundation\'s Defipunk criteria:

-   Permissionless access: anyone can interact with the core contracts without KYC or whitelisting.

-   Self-custody: users maintain custody at all times. The protocol holds principal in trust during circle participation, but withdrawal mechanics are enforced by code, not by a custodian.

-   Open source: all contract code is FLOSS-licensed and publicly auditable.

-   Privacy by default: balances, contribution history, and circle membership are shielded. The public ledger sees only cryptographic proofs of valid participation.

-   Governance with limits on financial influence: cooperative governance applies equal weight to all members regardless of deposit size. Protocol-level decisions are separated from circle-level decisions, each with appropriate governance scope.

-   Minimised oracle reliance: yield routing uses decentralised, manipulation-resistant oracle infrastructure for real-world rate data. The protocol is designed to continue functioning if any single data source fails.

## 7. The Broader Vision

Mandinga Protocol is not a stablecoin. It is not a yield aggregator. It is not a lending protocol. It is a new category: a solidarity savings primitive — infrastructure that encodes cooperative financial logic into immutable, permissionless code.

The success metric is not TVL. It is not APY. It is the number of people who accessed lump-sum capital earlier than they could have alone, the number of circles that completed without failure, and the degree to which financial access expanded to people previously excluded from any meaningful savings infrastructure.

The vision is a world where the ancient practice of community savings — the mechanism that has helped hundreds of millions of people build financial stability for millennia — is finally free from its structural vulnerabilities. Where a seamstress in Lagos, a delivery driver in São Paulo who knows exactly what a consórcio costs and why the bar should be higher, a developer in Buenos Aires, and a diaspora family across three continents can all participate in the same solidarity primitive, governed by the same cooperative logic, with the same privacy protections, and the same structural guarantees.

Finance that couldn\'t exist without Ethereum. Finance that the world has been trying to build for thousands of years and never quite could. Until now.
